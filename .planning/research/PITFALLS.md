## Critical Pitfalls

### Pitfall 1: Dark-photo banding turns the whole milestone's premium bet into a cheap one — the single highest-leverage risk in v3.0

**What goes wrong:**
A Milky Way/night-sky astrophotography image is, by nature, exactly the pathological case for banding: huge low-frequency gradient regions (deep graphite-to-black sky, soft nebula glow) with almost no high-frequency texture to mask quantization steps. Standard 8-bit-per-channel encoding gives only 256 luminance steps across the entire 0-255 range, but the *visible* range a viewer's eye can distinguish in a dark scene is compressed into a much smaller number of raw code values near black — banding shows up as discrete "false contour" rings/steps in smooth gradients, most visible in exactly this kind of large low-texture dark region. [MEDIUM — general imaging/compression literature, cross-referenced across BAND-2k/arXiv banding-artifact research and multiple encoder-comparison sources]. This is compounded twice over here: (1) lossy re-compression (AVIF/WebP) can *introduce or worsen* banding beyond what the source photo already has if quantization/quality settings aren't tuned for gradient-heavy content, and (2) this project's own composited-sky requirement (real photo base + a drawn constellation/meteor/moon overlay, per PROJECT.md's locked v3.0 direction) adds a second compositing pass (canvas `drawImage` blit + overlay draws) that can re-introduce or compound banding if the canvas itself isn't operating in a wide-enough internal precision path.

**Why it happens:**
Developers pick a "reasonable-looking" AVIF/WebP quality setting by eye on a bright test image, ship it, and never specifically inspect the darkest gradient regions at 100% zoom on a real 8-bit consumer display (banding is often invisible on a wide-gamut/10-bit reference monitor and glaringly visible on a cheap 6-bit-panel-plus-dithering laptop screen) [MEDIUM]. It also happens because "banding prevention" is treated as a single encode-time decision rather than an end-to-end pipeline concern spanning encode → delivery → display.

**How to avoid (the full pipeline, four stages):**
1. **Encode-time:** Prefer **AVIF** over WebP for this specific image — AVIF supports higher internal bit depth (10/12-bit) and its own quantization is specifically better-behaved on smooth gradients than WebP's, which several independent comparisons flag as *worse* than the source for exactly this content type [MEDIUM — encoder-comparison synthesis, not independently benchmarked in this environment; verify by generating both and doing the histogram test below on the actual sky image before locking the format]. AVIF's **film-grain synthesis (FGS)** feature is the single most relevant lever available: grain parameters are stored separately from the base image and re-synthesized at decode time, so a deliberately grain-added source encodes far smaller *and* the reconstructed grain itself is one of the most effective anti-banding techniques there is — texture breaks up the smooth gradient the eye would otherwise resolve into rings [MEDIUM — arXiv "Gain of Grain" VVC/AV1 grain-synthesis literature; MEDIUM confidence on exact AVIF FGS tooling maturity in the specific encoder this project will use (`sharp`/`libavif`) — verify FGS flag support in whatever CLI/`sharp` version is pinned before relying on it].
2. **Delivery-time (the "2x-resolution-lower-quality trick"):** Encoding the image at **2x its display resolution at a lower quality/bitrate**, then letting the browser downscale it on paint, is well-established practitioner wisdom: the downscale performs an implicit averaging pass across the 2x source pixels, smoothing quantization steps that would otherwise be visible at 1x. [MEDIUM — documented practitioner technique (game-dev/video-encoding communities, echoed in the dithered-gradient sources found) rather than a canonical spec source; validate empirically on this project's actual sky image, not a guaranteed fix]. Concretely: if the CSS-rendered sky is ~1920x1080 CSS px, deliver a 3840x2160 AVIF at a quality setting a few points lower than would look "hand-picked correct" at native res, and confirm the downscaled result is visibly *smoother*, not just smaller.
3. **Display-time (the noise-overlay safety net):** Regardless of source-encode cleanliness, layer a **subtle CSS or canvas noise/grain overlay** (mix-blend-mode `overlay`/`soft-light`, very low opacity, tiled) on top of the composited sky as last-mile insurance — the standard fix used across the CSS-Tricks "Grainy Gradients" pattern and several dithered-gradient libraries (`noisy-gradient`, SVG-filter approaches) found in research [MEDIUM, well-corroborated across independent sources]. This has two extra virtues here: (a) it doubles as part of the "real-life animation" ask if very subtly animated (faint drifting grain reads as atmospheric texture, not a bug), and (b) it's a display-side safety net independent of whatever the final AVIF encode settings turn out to be — cheap insurance against a banding regression introduced later by an unrelated asset-pipeline change.
4. **Canvas compositing precision:** Since the overlay (constellations/meteors/moon, carried forward from v2.0's `scene.ts`) draws on top of the photo via `drawImage`, verify the visible canvas's `getContext('2d')` isn't silently clamping to a lower-precision intermediate than the source AVIF's bit depth — Canvas2D contexts are 8-bit by default in every shipping browser, so **the base photo's extra bit depth is only useful up to the point it's decoded into the `<canvas>`**; if `scene.ts`'s existing `drawImage(layer0.canvas, ...)` blit pattern (unchanged since v2.0, confirmed at `src/lib/nightsky/scene.ts` line 334) is reused for the photo layer, the visible canvas is the final 8-bit bottleneck regardless of source encoding — meaning the noise-overlay step (③) is not optional, it's the primary anti-banding lever once the image lands in a standard 2D canvas context. [HIGH confidence on "Canvas2D is 8-bit" itself — documented browser behavior, not a research claim; MEDIUM on how much this specifically matters vs. compositing the *static* photo via plain CSS `background-image`/`<img>` instead of routing it through canvas — that alternative avoids the 8-bit bottleneck entirely for the base layer and is worth strongly considering as an architecture decision, flagged for the roadmap: only the drawn overlay (constellations/meteors/moon/fireflies) needs canvas; the photo itself does not. **This same architecture choice also resolves a separate, unrelated no-JS regression — see Pitfall 5's carry-forward item 1.**].

**How to TEST for banding objectively (not just eyeballing):**
- **Histogram analysis:** Export the darkest gradient region (e.g., a horizontal strip across the deep-sky area) and inspect its luminance histogram (`ImageMagick`, `Pillow`, or an editor's histogram panel) — a clean gradient histogram is smooth/continuous, while a banded one shows sharp, evenly-spaced spikes (a comb pattern) at quantization boundaries. This mirrors the "master vs. test set of local maxima" technique from banding-detection research (BAND-2k / banding-defect literature) — the practical DIY version is: histogram the *encoded/delivered* image and look for comb spikes absent from the *source* master's histogram. [MEDIUM — synthesized from banding-detection research papers; the underlying technique is sound and directly implementable with `ImageMagick`/`Pillow` without the papers' full deep-learning pipelines].
- **Screenshot-delta / automated regression test:** Capture a full-page screenshot of the rendered sky (Playwright/Puppeteer) and run the same histogram-comb check programmatically against a threshold, so a future asset-pipeline change (re-export, different quality, format swap) that reintroduces banding fails verification the same way the contrast floor does today. This directly extends the existing `scripts/verify-contrast.mjs` canvas-readback pattern already shipped in this codebase rather than inventing new tooling. [Project-specific recommendation, grounded in a real already-shipped pattern].
- **Real-device visual check is still required, not optional:** banding visibility is highly display-dependent (panel bit depth, OS-level dithering, GPU color management), so a histogram check that passes can still band visibly on a cheap laptop panel — budget an explicit manual check on at least one non-reference display (not a MacBook Pro) before calling this done, mirroring the "test on real mid-tier hardware" discipline already established in v2.0's PITFALLS.md Pitfall 7.

**Warning signs:**
Visible discrete rings/steps in the deep-sky gradient on a standard laptop display (especially external monitors/cheap panels, less so OLED phones); a histogram of the delivered image shows evenly-spaced comb spikes not present in the source master; banding appears *worse* after an AVIF/WebP re-encode than in the original high-bit-depth source (a strong signal the encoder's quantization needs retuning).

**Prevention owner (phase-level):** the image-acquisition/compositing phase, before any glass or animation work lands on top — banding baked into the base layer is the most expensive thing to discover late, since it affects the foundation every other layer sits on.

### Pitfall 2: A full-viewport photo hero is a direct hit on the project's best asset (Lighthouse 98-100 with zero images) — LCP risk is real but bounded and manageable if sequenced correctly

**What goes wrong:**
The current live site has **zero images** and LCP of 0.4-1.4s — that number is almost entirely a function of "there was nothing to wait for." The instant a full-viewport hero photo becomes an LCP candidate, this changes categorically, not incrementally. Two concrete failure modes: **(a) lazy-loading or missing `fetchpriority`/`preload`** — Chrome's own documented guidance flags that a `loading="lazy"` LCP image (or one the preload scanner can't discover early, e.g. a `background-image` set only via CSS rather than a plain `<img>`/`<link rel=preload>`) commonly costs **500ms+**, and adding `fetchpriority="high"` plus an explicit preload has produced **200-800ms** improvements in documented real cases (Google Flights: 700ms) [MEDIUM-HIGH — this range is repeated across several independent sources though the exact delta is case-specific]; **(b) the photo becomes LCP-competing with the hero panel's text** — a large full-viewport image is very likely to win Lighthouse's largest-element comparison over a modest text block, making the photo itself — not v1.0/v2.0's cheap serif hero text — the thing that now gates the LCP metric.

**Realistic LCP number to expect, and whether ≥90 mobile survives:**
GitHub Pages' Fastly-fronted delivery has no additional image-optimization layer, and GitHub Pages has historically set short cache-expiry windows on static assets (commonly cited as ~10 minutes) — meaning **cold-cache is the realistic Lighthouse test condition on effectively every run**, not just a true first-ever-visit [MEDIUM — corroborated across several independent sources synthesizing GitHub's documented caching behavior; verify empirically via `curl -I` on a live deployed asset before relying on it]. Given that, a well-optimized full-viewport AVIF (correctly sized, `fetchpriority="high"`, preloaded, never lazy-loaded, served over HTTP/2 from Fastly's edge) should realistically land **LCP in the ~1.5-2.8s range at mobile-preset (throttled) Lighthouse testing**, versus the current 0.4-1.4s zero-image baseline [LOW-MEDIUM — a reasoned estimate synthesized from general "well-optimized hero image" LCP ranges in sourced guidance, not a benchmark against this project's actual asset; flagged explicitly as an estimate requiring validation with a real Lighthouse run before the roadmap commits to a phase-level performance budget]. **Mobile Lighthouse performance ≥90 is NOT guaranteed to survive this.** Lighthouse's LCP scoring curve treats ~2.5s as the "good" ceiling with a steep drop-off past it, and LCP is one of the most heavily-weighted metrics in the overall performance score — landing at the upper end of the estimated range puts the category score at real risk even with every other metric excellent. This is the single most concrete "must re-verify before/after, don't assume" item for the roadmap, mirroring the v2.0 PITFALLS.md precedent (Pitfall 6) of Lighthouse-checkpointing after each heavy addition rather than only at the end.

**The low-quality-placeholder ladder, without CLS:**
The standard, well-documented pattern (LQIP/blur-up, as implemented by `next/image` and covered across the sourced guidance) is: **(1) solid color** (a CSS custom property matching the sky's dominant tone, paints instantly, zero network cost) → **(2) tiny blurred placeholder** (a ~20-40px-wide base64-embedded thumbnail inlined directly in HTML — zero extra round-trip — blurred via CSS `filter: blur()` at display size) → **(3) full image** crossfaded in on load. [HIGH confidence this three-rung ladder is the industry-standard pattern — corroborated independently across Cloudinary, Mux, CSS-Wizardry, and the Next.js reference implementation]. **CLS avoidance is structural, not optional**: the host must have its final position/size reserved before any rung paints. This project's existing architecture is already favorable here — `NightSky.astro`'s `.nightsky-host` (`position:fixed; inset:0; z-index:-1`) never participates in document flow, so compositing the photo into/behind that same fixed host carries zero CLS risk by construction, as long as the photo isn't introduced as a *new*, separately-flowed element. The one real CLS/flash risk is the rung 2→3 handoff if implemented via a raw `src` swap on an `<img>` — prefer stacking rung 2 and rung 3 as separate absolutely-positioned layers with a CSS opacity crossfade (both already correctly sized) over a `src`-attribute swap, which can briefly show a blank/broken state if not sequenced carefully.

**Prevention owner (phase-level):** the sky-image integration phase needs its own dedicated Lighthouse checkpoint (mobile *and* desktop, both presets) run immediately after the base photo lands, *before* any glass or overlay-animation work is layered on top — if LCP becomes the milestone's binding constraint, that needs to be known and budgeted early, not discovered during final QA.

### Pitfall 3: Photo + animating canvas + backdrop-filter glass in one stack invites four distinct, well-documented browser gotchas — the interaction that most threatens the idle-CPU floor

**What goes wrong (four sub-pitfalls, all directly applicable to this stack):**

1. **`backdrop-filter` forces a re-blur every single frame the content behind it changes** — and this project's whole premise is a *permanently animating* scene behind the glass. Browser rendering guidance is explicit: `backdrop-filter` blur reads pixels from whatever is currently behind the element, runs a blur kernel across them, and composites the result on every frame that source content changes; cost scales with both the blurred area and blur radius [MEDIUM-HIGH, corroborated across multiple independent rendering-performance sources]. Unlike a typical glassmorphism use case (a card floating over a *static* photo, where the blur is effectively computed once and cached), this project's glass panels sit over a scene that is *by design* always drawing something — twinkling stars, drifting fireflies, and in v3.0 additionally clouds/aurora/parallax/scintillation — meaning the backdrop-filter's expensive resample-and-reblur work cannot be short-circuited by browser caching the way most glassmorphism tutorials implicitly assume. This is precisely the interaction PROJECT.md's own "known tensions" section flags (backdrop-filter cost over large imagery) and is the primary mechanism that could blow the <10% idle-CPU floor even if the scene's *own* per-frame cost stays cheap, because now every glass panel adds its own resample-and-blur pass on top of the scene's draw cost, every frame, for as long as the scene keeps animating behind it.
2. **Safari backdrop-filter bugs are real and current (2024-2026), not legacy folklore** — multiple independent reports across Webflow/RapidWeaver/Apple-community forums document `backdrop-filter` + `background-color` failing to composite correctly together specifically on Safari 18 on iPhone, and nested `backdrop-filter` elements (a glass panel inside another glass region, e.g. a glass jump-index nested inside a glass panel-deck chrome) breaking in Safari specifically [MEDIUM — multiple independent forum/community reports, none a single authoritative root-cause writeup, but the pattern (nested backdrop-filter + Safari) recurs across unrelated sources]. `-webkit-backdrop-filter` must be specified alongside the unprefixed property for Safari support at all, and Safari's own Responsive Design Mode does not reliably emulate backdrop-filter — meaning DevTools device emulation during development can produce false confidence; real Safari/iOS hardware testing is required, not optional [HIGH confidence — this specific limitation of Safari's device-emulation tooling is a documented, testable fact].
3. **Mobile GPU compositing memory is a real ceiling, not just a "slow phones are slow" hand-wave** — a single 4096×4096 RGBA compositor layer already allocates 64MB uncompressed, and two or three such layers exhaust a mobile GPU's typical memory budget entirely [MEDIUM-HIGH, sourced from Chromium's own compositing architecture documentation]. `filter: blur()`/`backdrop-filter` force allocation of intermediate offscreen buffers per composited element, and a full-viewport photo *plus* a full-viewport animating canvas *plus* multiple glass panel layers (header/footer/jump-index/content panels, per PROJECT.md's "FULL glass system" scope) each potentially becoming their own compositor layer is a realistic way to stack several of these buffers simultaneously on a phone. Chrome's own diagnostics (`chrome://gpu`, `chrome://memory-internals`) report explicit GPU-process out-of-memory fallback to CPU-side rasterization when this ceiling is hit — which manifests as sudden, severe frame-rate collapse rather than a graceful degradation, and is invisible in desktop-Chrome testing where GPU memory budgets are far larger.
4. **iOS Low-Power-Mode throttles `requestAnimationFrame` to roughly 30fps with no reliable JS-detectable signal** — this directly affects `scene.ts`'s single rAF driver: the existing frame-delta clamp (`MAX_FRAME_DELTA_MS = 50`, confirmed in `scene.ts`) already defensively handles large frame gaps, which is good precedent, but the *visual* effect of a globally-halved frame rate (twinkle/firefly/aurora/cloud motion all reading as noticeably "steppier" under iOS Low Power Mode, which a meaningful fraction of real iPhone visitors have enabled, especially recruiters checking a portfolio on a conference-day-drained phone) is a real, undetectable-in-code UX risk, not just a performance number. [HIGH confidence on the throttling behavior itself — long-documented WebKit behavior, cited consistently from iOS 11-13 bug reports through current 2025-2026 community reports of the same behavior persisting; the WebKit Battery Status API needed to detect this was deprecated specifically because Apple does not want sites detecting/reacting to it, so there is no clean mitigation beyond designing animations that still read correctly at 30fps].

**z-index/isolation stacking traps specific to this codebase's stack:**
`backdrop-filter` (any value other than `none`) creates a new stacking context on the element it's applied to [HIGH — documented, standard CSS stacking-context-creation behavior]. This matters directly here because `NightSky.astro`'s existing negative-`z-index` trick (`.nightsky-host { z-index: -1 }`, deliberately negative rather than `0`, per that file's own extensive comment block, to paint correctly behind in-flow content in the no-JS/classic fallback) depends on painting-order rules that a misplaced `isolation: isolate` or an accidentally-`backdrop-filter`-ed ancestor of `.deck`/`.panel` could silently break — if any ancestor between `<body>` and a glass `.panel` picks up `backdrop-filter`/`transform`/`filter`/`will-change` (any of which independently create a stacking context) without a deliberate audit, the glass panel's `z-index` values stop competing in the context they're assumed to be in, and the classic `z-index: 9999`-style "just raise the number" instinct will not fix it — the fix is finding the ancestor that trapped the context and either removing the unintended property or adding a deliberate `isolation: isolate` boundary exactly where intended. Recommend a single documented "which elements in the v3.0 stack intentionally create a stacking context" list (photo host, canvas host, each type of glass surface) be part of the glass-system phase's own spec, mirroring `NightSky.astro`'s existing comment-block discipline of explaining *why* a z-index value is what it is.

**`prefers-reduced-transparency` — a floor v2.0 never needed, v3.0's glass system does:**
Windows, macOS, and iOS all ship a system-level "reduce transparency" accessibility preference (macOS: Accessibility → Display → Reduce Transparency; Windows: Personalization → Colors → Transparency effects; iOS: Accessibility → Display & Text Size → Reduce Transparency), and CSS now exposes this directly via `@media (prefers-reduced-transparency: reduce)` (Chrome 118+; MDN-documented) [HIGH — MDN + Chrome-for-Developers primary sources]. Non-opaque, blurred-background interfaces are a documented real accessibility problem independent of contrast-ratio math — some low-vision and cognitive-disability users find shifting, blurred backgrounds genuinely disorienting or headache-inducing even when the *measured* contrast ratio technically passes [MEDIUM, accessibility-advocacy source (Axess Lab), directionally consistent with the broader WCAG cognitive-load literature this project's v2.0 PITFALLS.md already leaned on]. The standard, low-effort mitigation: gate the glass panels' opacity/blur via a CSS custom property, and under `prefers-reduced-transparency: reduce`, drop blur to `none` and raise the panel's background opacity toward ~90-95% (effectively falling back to something close to v2.0's existing flat-scrim panel look) — this is a small, mechanical addition to the same media-query-branching discipline this codebase already applies rigorously to `prefers-reduced-motion` (see `scene.ts`'s `rm` handling) and should be treated as an equal-priority floor alongside it, not an afterthought, since PROJECT.md's locked v3.0 direction commits to glass on *every* chrome surface (header/footer/jump index/content panels), maximizing the number of surfaces this preference must correctly degrade.

**Warning signs:**
Idle CPU/GPU usage climbs measurably once glass panels are layered over the (already-animating) scene versus the flat-scrim v2.0 baseline, even with the scene's own draw cost unchanged; Safari-only visual bugs (missing blur, opaque-looking "glass," blur that only partially applies) that don't reproduce in Chrome or in Safari's own DevTools responsive-design-mode emulation; frame-rate collapses specifically on real mid-tier Android/older iPhone hardware that don't reproduce in desktop Chrome device emulation; a user with system-level "reduce transparency" enabled still sees full blur/translucency because the CSS branch was never added.

**Prevention owner (phase-level):** the glass-system phase needs (a) an explicit stacking-context audit as part of its own spec before implementation, (b) a real-Safari-hardware pass (not simulator/responsive-mode) before sign-off, (c) the `prefers-reduced-transparency` branch built in from the first glass component rather than retrofitted, and (d) an idle-CPU re-measurement specifically comparing "scene alone" vs. "scene + glass panels" so the marginal cost of glass is known, not just the combined total.

## Precedent Teardowns

**Method note (transparent about limitations):** text-only `WebFetch` reliably surfaces documented critiques/writeups and static-markup-observable sites, but does *not* reliably render or visually inspect JS-heavy, canvas/WebGL-driven award-site experiences (Awwwards SOTD galleries returned thumbnail/metadata listings, not usable design detail on fetch — noted as a failed/low-yield research path rather than silently dropped). The strongest, most concrete findings below come from a directly-fetched primary-source critique (Apple Liquid Glass) and two directly-fetched real production sites (ESO.org, Apple Vision Pro marketing page), supplemented by cross-corroborated glassmorphism-practitioner guidance for the numeric specifics (blur radius, opacity ceiling) no single fetch fully covered.

### 1. Apple "Liquid Glass" (iOS/iPadOS/macOS 26, 2025-2026) — the highest-profile, most-documented cautionary tale, not an aspirational model
[HIGH confidence — directly fetched primary-source critique from NN/G, a recognized UX-research authority, cross-referenced against the CSS-Tricks/DEV-community technical writeups found in the same research pass]
Apple's own system-wide glass UI is the most scrutinized glass-over-imagery deployment in the industry right now, and the documented failures are specific and directly relevant to v3.0's plan to glass *every* chrome surface: Messages text became "nearly invisible" when users set photo backgrounds behind translucent bubbles; Maps navigation icons blended into food/photo imagery in the background *despite* blurring; Mail's search bar and subject-line text created "cryptographically difficult to decipher" overlapping translucent text layers. NN/G's core diagnosis — "text on top of images is a bad idea because the contrast between the text and the background is often too low," and translucent elements "let background colors bleed through, creating variable contrast ratios that might work well over one background and fail over a bright [busy] one" — is a direct, worked example of exactly the risk this milestone's own locked floor (contrast ≥4.5:1 worst-case) is designed to catch, and confirms that **even Apple, with unlimited design/engineering resources, shipped this wrong at first.** **What to steal:** nothing from the failure mode itself — the lesson is entirely defensive. **What to steal structurally:** Apple's glass is adaptive/content-aware (it samples what's behind it and adjusts), which this project cannot replicate with static CSS `backdrop-filter` — meaning this project must compensate with a *fixed, deliberately-placed* scrim/opacity strategy (as v2.0 already does) rather than assuming blur alone will guarantee contrast anywhere a panel might sit, exactly the same discipline v2.0's PITFALLS.md Pitfall 8 already established for the flat-scrim system and which carries forward unchanged as a requirement for glass.

### 2. ESO.org (European Southern Observatory) — real astrophotography content, deliberately NO glass-over-photo for body text
[HIGH confidence — directly fetched]
ESO is one of the world's actual sources of the kind of astrophotography imagery this milestone wants to use, and its own site's design choice is telling: hero/feature imagery is used prominently, but **body text is never overlaid directly on busy photo regions** — it lives in separate, spatially-distinct card containers with their own backgrounds, and no frosted-glass/translucent effect was observed anywhere in the fetched markup. **What to steal:** the "spatial separation over overlay" pattern — for the parts of v3.0's content that are dense reading (case-study-equivalent body copy, the systems/experience panels with paragraph-length text), consider whether glass-with-heavy-blur is actually the right call versus a more opaque, clearly-separated card that merely *gestures* at glass (hairline edge, subtle translucency) without relying on blur to carry legibility — this is consistent with PROJECT.md's own "editorial restraint" design doctrine established in v1.0, and suggests the "FULL glass system" locked direction should still bias toward higher opacity / lower blur specifically wherever paragraph-length text lives, reserving the most transparent, most blurred glass treatment for lower-text-density chrome (header/footer/jump index) — exactly where the numeric guidance below also points.

### 3. General glassmorphism practitioner consensus (cross-corroborated across multiple independent 2025-2026 design guides) — concrete numeric defaults worth adopting as starting points
[MEDIUM confidence — synthesized across several independent practitioner sources (not project-specific benchmarks); treat as a reasonable starting point to tune from, not a locked spec]
- **Blur radius sweet spot: 10-20px.** Below ~8px reads as "slightly out of focus" rather than "frosted"; above ~30px starts looking like fog rather than glass.
- **Background opacity ceiling: ~0.2 (20%) if blur alone is meant to carry the "glass" look; push higher (0.25+) only once a scrim/text-shadow is added for contrast**, since past ~0.25 opacity the glass *effect* itself visually disappears (the panel starts reading as a plain solid color) — meaning "raise opacity for readability" and "keep it looking like glass" are in direct tension, and the resolution most sources converge on is *add a scrim or text-shadow*, not *keep raising opacity*, when contrast is the problem.
- **`saturate(150-200%)` alongside the blur** restores the chromatic depth pure blur otherwise desaturates/greys out, making the glass read as "premium" rather than "washed out" — directly relevant to this project's copper-accent/graphite palette, since a desaturated glass panel could visually mute the `--accent` copper tone this project's whole design system is built around.
- **The one converging real-world pattern across every credible source found (Rains-style e-commerce sites, the Apple critique, ESO's restraint):** glass is used sparingly and deliberately on **chrome/interactive elements (nav, buttons)**, not blanket-applied to every text-bearing surface — which is worth surfacing as a direct tension with PROJECT.md's locked "FULL glass system... panels, header/footer, and jump index all become frosted-glass surfaces" direction. This isn't a reason to override the user's locked decision, but it is a strong signal that **panel body-text glass and header/footer/jump-index glass should very likely use different blur/opacity tuning**, not one uniform glass recipe applied everywhere — flagged as a design-phase decision point, not assumed away.

### 4-5. Space-themed glassmorphism galaxy/nebula sites and Awwwards "big background image" gallery — low-yield research path, noted honestly
Search surfaced named live sites (e.g., a "Cosmic Glassmorphism Portfolio" starfield-hero + glass-card layout, several Webflow-community glass-over-photo templates) and an Awwwards big-background-image gallery, but `WebFetch`'s text-only rendering could not extract meaningful legibility/load-strategy/motion detail from these (gallery/listing pages return thumbnails and titles, not renderable page content; individual JS-heavy sites weren't independently fetchable within this pass). **This is flagged as a genuine research gap rather than papered over** — recommend the roadmap budget a short manual/visual precedent review (a human or a screenshot-capable tool actually opening 2-3 of these named candidates) before the visual-design phase locks the glass system's exact blur/opacity numbers, since the strongest evidence gathered here (Apple's failure, ESO's restraint, cross-corroborated numeric defaults) is directionally solid but not a substitute for looking at an actual space-photography-plus-glass site rendered in a real browser.

## Regression Surface — what v3.0 touches vs. what must not break

**Carry-forward verification list (lift directly into the roadmap):**

- [ ] **No-JS/classic mode renders the photo.** If the sky photo is implemented as a canvas `drawImage` (mirroring the existing Layer-0 pattern), it will be **completely absent** under no-JS, since `initNightSky` never runs without JS — the classic/no-JS fallback would show a flat background where the photo should be. Resolve by delivering the *static* photo via plain CSS `background-image`/`<img>` (renders with zero JS) and reserving canvas exclusively for the *drawn* overlay (constellations/meteors/moon/fireflies/scintillation), which is allowed to be JS-only since it was already JS-only in v2.0. This is the single highest-priority carry-forward item — it's a hard functional regression, not a polish issue, and it doubles as Pitfall 1's recommended anti-banding architecture (item 4).
- [ ] **Every new ambient system (clouds/haze, aurora, scintillation, parallax) rides `scene.ts`'s single rAF tick and its existing `updateRunState()` gate** — no new system gets its own `requestAnimationFrame` call, its own `visibilitychange` listener, or its own reduced-motion check. Verify by grep: the count of `requestAnimationFrame(` calls in `src/lib/nightsky/*` must stay at exactly the number already established (one, in `scene.ts`), and the count of `nightsky:panel-change`/`visibilitychange`/`prefers-reduced-motion` listeners must not grow beyond the existing owners (`scene.ts` for the pause gate, `constellations.ts` for its own brighten/dim, per the documented "two independent listeners" precedent) plus whatever the new parallax-on-panel-change feature explicitly needs.
- [ ] **Re-run the full v2.0 36-check Fig. 01 embedded-panel audit** once the glass system and photo land, with explicit attention to whether Fig. 01's own panel inherits a text-tuned glass/blur treatment that degrades its canvas's own legibility — Fig. 01 may need an explicit exemption from the generic `.panel` glass styling.
- [ ] **Decide and document the case-study/404 header-footer glass strategy** before implementation: either (a) extend a (possibly simplified/static) version of the sky photo behind shared chrome on every page, or (b) give `SiteHeader`/`SiteFooter` a documented "glass over nothing" fallback recipe (higher opacity, no reliance on blur reading as glass) for scene-free pages — currently undecided territory, not covered by v2.0 precedent since v2.0's scrim never touched shared chrome.
- [ ] **Extend or replace `verify-contrast.mjs`'s methodology for glass panels** — the existing analytic-alpha composite is provably insufficient for real `backdrop-filter` blur (confirmed from its own source comments); either extend the `--cdp` mode to read the *actually rendered, actually blurred* pixels via a real screenshot/canvas-of-the-page technique, or explicitly document the analytic approximation's known error margin for blurred panels before trusting its worst-case number.
- [ ] **Photo credit/attribution line renders in static HTML**, reachable and visible identically in deck mode, classic mode, and no-JS mode — verify via `curl`/JS-disabled fetch, mirroring the same check v2.0's PITFALLS.md Pitfall 9 already established for case-study crawlability.
- [ ] **Re-verify the `/#work` alias and cold-`/#fig-01`-deep-link pause fix** (both shipped in 06-02) still resolve correctly once the hash-routing surface hasn't changed but the *visual* surface around it has — regression risk is low here since `deck.ts` isn't planned to change, but it's a cheap, fast re-check given how recently (and painfully) these two were fixed.
- [ ] **Sitemap/OG/robots.txt unchanged** after the new image asset(s) land — a quick sitemap-URL-count and OG-image-render diff against the pre-v3.0 baseline.

## Verdict — Top-10 Ranked Pitfalls

Ranked by **(severity if unaddressed) × (likelihood of being missed without deliberate process)**, each with its primary prevention and confidence tag. Items 1-3 map to this research's three main sections; items 4-10 are the sharpest specific sub-risks and regression items pulled out of that analysis.

1. **Dark-photo banding (Pitfall 1).** Prevention: AVIF + film-grain synthesis at encode time, 2x-resolution delivery trick, a display-time noise overlay as a mandatory safety net (not optional), and route the *static* photo through CSS/`<img>` rather than an 8-bit Canvas2D context. Test via histogram comb-spike detection extending `verify-contrast.mjs`'s existing pattern, plus a mandatory real-device check on a non-reference display. [MEDIUM overall — no single canonical source, but the four-stage pipeline is corroborated across independent, credible sources at each stage; the Canvas2D-is-8-bit fact itself is HIGH]. Sources: [BAND-2k arXiv](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2311.17752), [Gain of Grain arXiv](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2402.00622), [CSS-Tricks Grainy Gradients](https://css-tricks.com/grainy-gradients/), [Fix Gradient Banding in WebP](https://www.shadcn.io/tools/fix-gradient-banding-webp).

2. **No-JS/classic mode loses the photo entirely if it's canvas-drawn (Pitfall 5 carry-forward item 1).** This is a hard functional regression against the shipped DECK-07 no-JS floor, not a polish nit — ranked this high because it's easy to miss (the JS-happy-path developer never disables JS while testing) and total when it happens. Prevention: deliver the static photo via plain CSS/`<img>`, reserve canvas for the drawn overlay only. [Project-specific, grounded directly in this codebase's own architecture (`initNightSky` only runs via a `<script>` module) — HIGH confidence this is a real risk given the architecture, though it's a logical inference from reading the code rather than an external source].

3. **LCP regression threatens the mobile Lighthouse ≥90 floor (Pitfall 2).** Prevention: `fetchpriority="high"` + `<link rel=preload>`, never lazy-load the hero photo, a solid-color→blurred-thumbnail→full LQIP ladder anchored to the existing zero-CLS `.nightsky-host` pattern, and a dedicated Lighthouse checkpoint immediately after the photo lands, before glass/animation work compounds the risk. [MEDIUM-HIGH on the general fetchpriority/preload deltas; LOW-MEDIUM on the specific 1.5-2.8s estimate for this project, flagged as needing empirical validation]. Sources: [Total Blocking Time — Chrome for Developers](https://developer.chrome.com/docs/lighthouse/performance/lighthouse-total-blocking-time), [CSS-Wizardry LQIP/LCP technique](https://csswizardry.com/2023/09/the-ultimate-lqip-lcp-technique/).

4. **`backdrop-filter` re-blurring a permanently-animating scene, every frame, on every glass panel (Pitfall 3, sub-pitfall 1).** This is the single most direct mechanism that could blow the <10% idle-CPU floor, and it's structurally different from how almost all glassmorphism tutorials/precedents are built (glass over a *static* image). Prevention: minimize simultaneously-composited glass regions, keep blur radii in the 10-20px range rather than pushing higher, and explicitly re-measure idle CPU with glass layered on vs. off as a distinct verification step. [MEDIUM-HIGH, corroborated across multiple independent rendering-performance sources]. Sources: [cr0x.net CSS Animation Performance](https://cr0x.net/en/css-animations-performance-rules/), [Real Cost of Animations — Medium](https://robertcelt95.medium.com/the-real-cost-of-animations-performance-budget-vs-user-delight-227199cf5d27).

5. **The contrast-verification tool itself can't correctly validate glass (Pitfall 5 carry-forward item, from direct read of `verify-contrast.mjs`).** A false-green contrast floor is worse than a known-red one — if the analytic-alpha verifier reports "pass" for a blurred glass panel it never actually blurred in its simulation, the ≥4.5:1 worst-case floor becomes unverified precisely where it matters most (the new, riskiest surface). Prevention: extend `--cdp` mode to sample real rendered/blurred pixels, or explicitly bound the analytic approximation's error before trusting it. [Project-specific — HIGH confidence this gap exists, grounded in direct source read; MEDIUM on exact remediation approach].

6. **Safari-specific backdrop-filter bugs, invisible in DevTools emulation (Pitfall 3, sub-pitfall 2).** Prevention: real Safari/iOS hardware testing (not Responsive Design Mode), `-webkit-backdrop-filter` alongside the unprefixed property, avoid nesting glass-in-glass (e.g. jump-index inside deck chrome) without an explicit real-device check. [MEDIUM — multiple independent but non-authoritative community reports; HIGH confidence specifically that Safari's device emulation doesn't reliably reproduce backdrop-filter, which is the actionable process implication]. Sources: [Apple Community thread on backdrop-filter + Safari 18](https://discussions.apple.com/thread/255764118).

7. **Case-study/404 pages get glass header/footer with nothing to blur behind them (Pitfall 5 carry-forward item, from direct read of the page-composition files).** A previously-invisible architecture decision — v2.0's scrim never touched shared chrome, so this is genuinely new territory for v3.0, not a v2.0 pattern to extend. Prevention: decide explicitly (extend photo to all pages vs. a documented "glass over nothing" fallback recipe) before implementation, not during. [Project-specific, HIGH confidence the gap exists — directly confirmed by reading `work/[slug].astro`'s imports].

8. **`prefers-reduced-transparency` is a genuinely new floor v2.0 never needed and could easily ship without (Pitfall 3).** Low effort to add correctly if built in from the first glass component (mirrors the existing `prefers-reduced-motion` branching discipline exactly), high user-real cost if missed (documented disorientation/headache risk for a real accessibility population, independent of measured contrast). [HIGH on the media query's existence/behavior (MDN/Chrome docs); MEDIUM on the disorientation-risk framing (accessibility-advocacy source)]. Sources: [MDN prefers-reduced-transparency](https://developer.mozilla.org/en-US/docs/Web/CSS/Reference/At-rules/@media/prefers-reduced-transparency), [Chrome for Developers blog](https://developer.chrome.com/blog/css-prefers-reduced-transparency).

9. **Mobile GPU compositing-memory ceiling from stacking photo + canvas + multiple glass layers (Pitfall 3, sub-pitfall 3).** Manifests as sudden severe frame-rate collapse rather than graceful degradation, and is invisible in desktop-Chrome testing. Prevention: real mid-tier Android device testing (not device emulation), minimize simultaneous transparent/blurred compositor layers, watch `chrome://gpu`/`chrome://memory-internals` during development. [MEDIUM-HIGH, sourced from Chromium's own compositing architecture documentation]. Sources: [Chromium Graphics developer docs](https://www.chromium.org/developers/design-documents/chromium-graphics/).

10. **Glass applied uniformly to every surface (header/footer/jump-index/body-text panels) may fight legibility on the highest-text-density surfaces specifically** (Precedent Teardown #2-3 synthesis: ESO's restraint + cross-corroborated practitioner guidance that glass belongs on chrome, not body text). Not a hard technical bug, but a design-quality risk directly informed by the strongest precedent evidence gathered (Apple's own documented failure mode was exactly "text over busy translucent background"). Prevention: bias toward higher-opacity/lower-blur glass specifically where paragraph-length text lives (systems/experience/case-study-equivalent panels), reserving the most transparent/most-blurred treatment for low-text chrome — a design-phase decision to make deliberately, not a locked-direction override. [MEDIUM, synthesized across multiple independent practitioner and precedent sources]. Sources: [NN/G Liquid Glass usability](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/liquid-glass/), [Smashing Magazine — text over images](https://www.smashingmagazine.com/2023/08/designing-accessible-text-over-images-part1/) (carried forward from v2.0's own PITFALLS.md sourcing, still directly applicable).
